    The Propella 7S Is a Better-Looking Budget E-bike

    Andre Yulianto January 12, 2024

    I love the Propella 7S. I’ve ridden more powerful e-bikes. I’ve ridden faster, more versatile e-bikes—rides that are three times as expensive with five times the features. But if I’m sticking to paved roads and just tooling around the neighborhood, the Propella 7S is my top choice.

    It may lack the bells (literally) and whistles of higher-end rides, but the 7S feels like a bike, not a moped. It performs its one, simple task—assisted riding on paved roads—surprisingly well. It currently retails for less than $799, and it looks stylish-as-hell.

    Pros and cons of the Propella 7S

    Pros

    • Low price

    • Amazing looks

    • Lightweight

    Cons

    • Lack of versatility

    • Low power will leave some people wanting more

    Specs

    • Classification: Class 1

    • Motor: Bafang 250W, 5-gear, rear hub

    • Weight limit: 230 lbs.

    • Assist: 5-levels of pedal assist

    • Battery: removable 36V 7Ah (250 Wh) Samsung

    • Recharge time: 2.5 hours

    • Advertised max range: 40 miles

    • Display: LCD display: speedometer, odometer, trip meter, clock.

    • Brakes: Shimano mechanical disc brakes

    • Drivetrain: Shimano Altus 7-speed

    • Tires: CST Xpedium 700 x 35c tires

    The Propella 7S is a surprisingly stylish e-bike

    Credit: Stephen Johnson

    “

    The 7S is downright sexy—an adjective rarely applied to e-bikes.

    ”

    Maybe I’m shallow, but when it comes to bikes, looks matter, and the 7S looks hot. The 7S’s 250W engine isn’t going to win a tug-of-war with a beefier ride, but in a beauty contest, it’s definitely going to take home the trophy. Between the matte black aluminum frame with classic bike-geometry, the white chain, the snazzy anodized electric blue rims on the 28-inch skinny wheels, and the matching blue accents on the grips and battery, the 7S is downright sexy—an adjective rarely applied to e-bikes.

    Style-wise, the only detraction is the removable battery affixed to the bar. It looks out-of-place and outdated. A battery built into the frame might be too much to ask on such an inexpensive ride, but I wish Propella sold a cover that disguised it as a water bottle or something.

    How does the 7S ride?

    All the looks in the world don’t mean a thing if it ain’t got that schwing, So how does the 7S ride? Overall, better than I expected.

    This is a class 1 e-bike, with no throttle control and an assisted top speed of 18.5 mph, so it’s not going to leave anyone in the dust, but unlike many faster e-bikes, the 7S is not an electric moped with crank-able pedals to comply with the law; it’s very much a bike. It weighs only 37 pounds, light enough for most car-mounted bike racks and is perfectly rideable without pedal assist.

    The frame geometry, rider position, and wheel-size will all be familiar to analog bikers, so riding it feels like riding a “normal” bike. It doesn’t handle like a Tour-de-France-ready whip or anything, but it’s appropriately agile and responsive on paved roads, and it doesn’t feel cheap, despite the low sticker price. Overall, it’s fun, in the popping-up-a-curb, leaning into the corners, riding-with-no-hands spirit of casual, tooling-around-town cycling.

    Credit: Stephen Johnson

    It’s not designed for gravel or dirt, so there’s no suspension to dampen shocks, but it’s not a stiff ride either. The tires ride at a fairly low pressure and there’s enough flex in the frame so you don’t feel your teeth rattling or anything.

    The pedal assist is solid, especially for such an inexpensive ride. It’s not as smooth as more advanced cadence sensing pedal assist, but it provides the oomph you need when you need it, without that unsettling, too-much-torque-on-the-start feeling some bikes give.

    I’ve ridden the 7S over 50 miles so far, on all different kinds of paved roads, at all levels of pedal assist, and had no issues. The lower settings work great for bringing you up to speed with a lighter touch, and at higher settings, the motor surges to life with a rotation of the pedals.

    To put the 7S to the test, I rode up a 1,200 foot hill at the highest level of assist, pedaling as infrequently as possible. While this burned a lot of the battery, it still hauled me up there without conking out.

    Speaking of the battery: The manufacturer says the 7s can go up to 40 miles per charge. But how long it will actually go depends on so many other factors—hills, rider weight, speed, wind, etc—that it’s impossible to really tell. I can say that the 7S seems to provide a reasonable amount of charge for most purposes. It’s not going to take you cross-country, but it will take you across town. Recharge takes about 2.5 hours.

    Controls and display

    Credit: Stephen Johnson

    There is nothing fancy about the 7S’s controls.. There’s an on-switch, and an up-button and down button to control the pedal assist level. The LCD display is similarly simple: It shows you how fast you’re going, how many miles you’ve traveled, what pedal assist level you’re using and the time of day. In other words, it’s about as easy-to-use as possible.

    In keeping with many e-bikes’ battery indicators (and as a byproduct of lithium battery power as a whole) the gauge on the 7S varies wildly depending on how much load the engine is under at the moment you’re looking, so a nearly empty gauge going up a hill might seem to fill up to three quarters as you coast on the way down. It’s the kind of thing you have to get used to.

    Maintenance and repair

    I had no mechanical problems in my time with this bike—the gears shift smoothly, the brakes work great, and there are no unsettling rattling noises. But I haven’t owned it long enough to speak to its longterm reliability. The parts from manufacturers like Shimano and CST suggest quality, and that the old-school parts of this bike are standard means you can repair it yourself or take it to the local bike shop.

    The bottom line

    If you’re in the market for a less expensive e-bike, or you’re a beginner who wants to try one out the Propella 7s should be at the top of your must-consider list.
