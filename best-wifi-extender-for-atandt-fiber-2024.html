    Factors To Consider When Looking For A WiFi Extender For AT&T Fiber

    WiFi Coverage 

    The main reason for purchasing a WiFi extender is to boost coverage within the home because your existing router setup is unable to reach all areas. WiFi extenders connect to the main AT&T router and generate a new WiFi access point where users log in separately.

    The coverage, expressed in square feet, refers to the total area that a signal from a WiFi extender can theoretically reach.

    WiFi extenders can generate coverage ranging from 1,200 sq. ft to 2,800 sq. ft. Generally, products offering greater coverage typically cost more.

    When we say that the coverage advertised is theoretical, we mean that it is an estimate and does not factor nuances within the home such as the location, presence of thick walls, or obstacles that can reduce home network coverage.

    The coverage generated by a WiFi extender is also dependent on the quality of the router it is being paired with.

    A router that generates a weak or poor-quality signal, will also have a weak extended signal.  

    When reviewing products for their coverage, you should assume that an extender can reach anywhere between 70% and 120% of the advertised coverage.

    WiFi Speed (WiFi 5 vs WiFi 6)

    The majority of routers on the market are designed with WiFi 5 or WiFi 6 standards. WiFi 5 routers are universally compatible with internet-using devices, cheaper, and are best for lower-speed internet plans of up to 500 Mbps.

    WiFi 6 includes advanced security and performance features, supports more devices as well, and provides faster and more reliable internet speeds. WiFi 6 routers are equipped to operate on gigabit internet plans.

    When looking for the best WiFi extender to suit your household setup, you should be looking to align the extender with the WiFi standard of your router to enable optimal performance.

    If you own a router operating with WiFi 5, you don’t need to purchase a WiFi 6 compatible extender.

    If you are unsure about which standard your router operates, check underneath your router and see if the protocol is displayed. WiFi 5 will display 802.11ac, while WiFi 6 will show 802.11ax.

    Number Of Supported Devices 

    Similar to routers, WiFi extenders will also present the number of devices the extender can connect to at any one time.

    If there are more devices connected to the extender than the figure presented, the signal reliability and speed will drop.

    The majority of WiFi Extenders can handle up to 30 connected devices, but some can handle more than 50.

    These figures are estimated and assume low-consumption usage across the connected devices.

    To work out which is the ideal number of connected devices you need for a WiFi extender, first work out the total number of internet-using devices in your household, then add 10% to 20% premium to arrive at the figure.

    WiFi Extender vs Mesh WiFi System

    If you’re weighing up investing in a WiFi extender or a mesh WiFi system, you need to understand the main trade-offs.

    In terms of coverage, practicality, and reliability, mesh systems are superior to WiFi Extenders as the mesh router and nodes form a unified network which allows users to log in to one household network and have their connections automatically passed between router and nodes to ensure you are connected to the nearest WiFi access point.

    WiFi extenders are more affordable and easier to setup compared to mesh systems as they typically consist of a single plug-in unit that then connects to your existing router.

    This is ideal for those who want to boost coverage without needing to significantly change their existing WiFi setup.

    In terms of usability and performance, WiFi extenders are less practical as users will need to log in separately to the extended network whenever they are in range.

    Also, an extender can use up to 50% total network bandwidth which can reduce the quality of the internet connection.

    We recommend WiFi extenders are best for households who are happy with their existing setup but want to extend coverage to a specific area of the house.

    Mesh systems are targeted at households that are larger than 2,000 sq. ft and therefore need the advanced technology offered by a mesh system to reach all parts of the home.

    FAQs

    How Do You Set Up A WiFi Extender With AT&T Fiber?  

    To set up a WiFi extender with AT&T Fiber, first, download the app from the app store or access the interface via att.com/smarthomemanager.

    Next, log in and select the Network. After this, click the Home Network Hardware and select the Add Extender icon.

    Then identify and select the Extender mode you are using and then follow the prompts to complete the setup.

    Once connected, place the Extender in the most strategic location in your home to improve network coverage.

    Should I Get A WiFi Extender Or A Mesh WiFi System? 

    Mesh systems perform better in terms of reliable internet speeds and coverage but they are expensive, ranging from $200 to $500 and disrupt your existing household internet setup.

    We recommend a mesh system only for properties larger than 2,000 sq. ft.

    For smaller properties, a WiFi extender should suffice in terms of extending your coverage with the home.

    Extenders are affordably priced, ranging from $50 to $80, and can be easily connected to your existing router.

    Are All WiFi Extenders Compatible With AT&T Fiber?

    All WiFi extenders should work with AT&T Fiber so compatibility should not be a significant issue.

    When reviewing a product, double-check that there are no compatibility issues with fiber internet.

    Will A WiFi Extender Slow Down My Internet Speed? 

    WiFi extenders do not directly slow down the internet speed received from the internet service provider.

    That said, WiFi extenders do affect bandwidth, as they can use up to 50% of total network bandwidth once connected. This in turn can impact the speed and quality of the internet that is accessed by users.
