    How Can I Restore Closed Tabs After Accidentally Quitting Chrome?

    Andre Yulianto January 12, 2024

    You know what we’re talking about: That sinking feeling you get when you realize the desktop version of Chrome has quit or crashed with 42 open tabs. And when you attempt to use the “Restore” option, you receive...nothing. Goodbye, work; hello internal screaming.

    Thankfully, Google has made it relatively easy to recover what you were working on in the event that this first and most obvious tool can’t help you out—and there are also plenty of third-party extensions or utilities you can use to protect yourself before your browser crashes (or you quit out of it).

    Check your Chrome history

    Credit: David Murphy

    When you reopen Chrome, click on the triple-dot menu in the upper-right corner and select “History.” You should see a number of sites under “Recently Closed” that correspond to each window you had open. You can also pull up your full history; search through it a bit and look for the moments when you launched Chrome for the first time after closing it (for example, the large gap of time you weren’t using Chrome while you slept). All of your previously open tabs should be listed above that breakpoint, assuming you’ve remembered to....

    Always enable “Continue where you left off”

    Credit: Emily Long

    Under Chrome > Settings you’ll find an option to direct Chrome to relaunch all previously open tabs on startup. Scroll down to the bottom of your settings page and select “Continue where you left off.” While this won’t stop a browser crash from potentially nuking your collection of open sites, it’s a quick and easy way to maintain an active workspace—as in, a healthy list of tabs overwhelming the top of your browser’s window.

    Memorize this quick keyboard shortcut

    A simple keyboard shortcuts can relaunch accidentally closed tabs. Press CTRL + Shift + T on a PC and Command + Shift + T on a Mac. This won’t bring back an entire browser’s worth of sites that have disappeared, but it’s a quick way to restore something you might have just closed accidentally.

    Prevent yourself from closing your browser too early

    If you find yourself accidentally quitting your browser often and you’re a Mac user, enable the Warn Before Quitting feature (under the Chrome menu) and use Command + Q to quit. This prompts you to hold the keyboard shortcut before Chrome will actually close, making you less likely to accidentally lose all of your tabs.

    Get some extensions—or a digital magnifying glass.

    If you have an out-of-control tab situation, there are plenty of extensions to help you organize and save your tabs. Our favorite is Session Buddy, which has saved the day more times than we can count.

    Otherwise, if you’ve closed your browser and have no history to search (or too much of one), and haven’t set up any of these other techniques, you’ll have to climb into the weeds. As Tessa Miller wrote for Lifehacker back in 2013:

    [Look for] the following four files in your User Data Directory:

    • Current Session

    • Current Tabs

    • Last Session

    • Last Tabs

    Copy these four files somewhere so that you can work without losing anything. That way, you can always copy them back if something goes wrong.

    ...you can manually extract the tabs from the files by using a string-searching program like Strings (go figure). Run the program, passing it the files and save them to another file. Most such programs require using the command-line, but presumably there are GUI versions as well (though I can’t seem to find any at the moment). So for example, if you save strings.exe toc:\foobar and copy the four files to there as well, you could extract the tabs as so:

    C:\foobar>strings “current tabs” > ctabs.txt

    C:\foobar>strings “last tabs” > ltabs.txt

    C:\foobar>strings “current session” > csess.txt

    C:\foobar>strings “last session” > lsess.txt

    Now you can open the new *tabs.txt text files and see the URLs of the tabs you had open before. Note that you may see a lot of duplication as well as some tabs that you had closed.

    The *sess.txt files will contain other information like text that you had typed into forms on a page, so if you had a page open with a bunch of information that you had entered, you can recover that so that you don’t have to start all over again from scratch. (For example, if I accidentally close this page, I could recover this text that I am typing at this very moment instead of trying to do it all again. The horror!)

    You can also try making another copy of “Last Session” and “Last Tabs,” renaming the files to “Current Session” and “Current Tabs,” and overwriting the existing “Current Session” and “Current Tabs” files in your User Data Directory—assuming your browser just crashed or closed and you need to restore that immediate, previous session.
