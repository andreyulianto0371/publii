    Things To Consider When Choosing The Best WiFi Router For An Apartment

    Speed

    When searching for the best router for an apartment, the WiFi speed is a key consideration.

    Generally, WiFi speed is most impacted by the WiFi protocol that the router uses. The most common standards used today are WiFi 5, WiFi 6, WiFi 6E and WiFi 7. For most small to medium-sized apartments, either a WiFi 5 or WiFi 6 product will suffice.

    WiFi 5 (802.11ac) has been available since 2014 and is best used on lower-speed internet plans of 500 Mbps and below. WiFi 5 routers are universally compatible with most client devices, and are affordably priced.

    WiFi 6 (802.11ax) routers can handle gigabit internet plans are equipped with advanced features such as Beamforming and QoS that improve signal quality and enable effective device management by assigning bandwidth to high-consumption users.

    WiFi 6E (802.11ax) advances on WiFi 6 in terms of speed by adding the 6 GHz frequency band which uses ultra-wide 320 MHz channels, to deliver up to 10 Gbps in WiFi speed.

    The issue with WiFi 6E is that it is not backward compatible, meaning you need WiFi 6E client devices to use the network effectively.

    WiFi 7 (802.11be) is the latest wireless protocol to be released and delivers up to 40 Gbps internet speed.

    Due to their recent release, WiFi 7 units are very expensive and there are not many client devices nor internet plans available to utilize the ultra-fast speed.  

    WiFi Range & Coverage

    The WiFi range and coverage is crucial as you want to access your internet network from every corner of your apartment.

    In the US, the average apartment size is 887 sq.ft. For most apartment dwellers, therefore, a router that offers between 1,000 and 2,000 sq.ft should be reasonable.

    The router coverage advertised by a manufacturer is theoretical as it does not include specific nuances within an apartment.

    Considering this, you should invest in a router that exceeds the square footage of your apartment so that you can access the network throughout the property.

    For apartments that are larger than 2,000 sq.ft, we recommend investing in a mesh system as this will enable you to seamlessly extend coverage.

    Smaller properties can use either a WiFi Extender or Wireless Access Point to extend coverage.

    To boost coverage within your apartment you should ensure that the router is located centrally and has no obstructions in its way.

    Also, coverage is greatly impacted by the frequency band with the 2.4 GHz offering much improved coverage compared to the 5 GHz or 6 GHz bands.

    Dual Band vs Tri-Band

    These days most routers have at least 2 bands, 5GHz, and 2.4GHz. 5GHz is quicker but does not cover a wide area while 2.4GHz has a slower speed but covers a wider range.

    On most routers, a 5GHz band covers a medium-sized apartment as you can get quicker speeds in a couple of bedrooms. However, you can always use a WiFi extender to upgrade the distance if you need it.

    On the other hand, tri-band routers have a third band of 5GHz. This can be great for apartments because if two 5GHz are available, it frees up congestion, allowing more devices to get better performance.

    Tri-band could be worthwhile if you stream regularly, or if you live with many people.

    However, tri-band routers are typically more expensive than dual-band, so assess your budget before committing to a tri-band router.

    Ports

    The availability of ports on a router may influence your purchasing decision.

    The ports are typically located at the rear or side of a router. These typically include WAN, LAN for Ethernet, and a USB port.

    Nowadays routers with USB ports are used for affordable network storage.

    You can plug a flash storage drive or an HDD into your router and share the data with other devices on the network.

    You can also use the disk to create your network media hub for streaming music, movies, or local television shows.

    Router Features

    The following are the features of a router that you should know:

    QoS means Quality of Service and enables data prioritization on the network. This happens by pressing the button on the router’s control panel and choosing prioritized activities including gaming, working from home, and streaming.

    Beamforming is suitable for larger apartments because it improves the router signal range and quality.

    Parental control enables parents to control their children’s activities when using the internet. They can block some topics or URLs, and this protects the kids from accessing inappropriate content.

    MU-MIMO improves your ability to simultaneously talk to multiple devices. It needs a home with smart devices that are constantly communicating with the router.

    The guest network enables the setup of an extra network with limited access. This improves your security because your guest cannot share the same network as your phone.

    Where To Place Your Router for The Best WiFi Signal

    Positioning your router in an optimum spot ensures that there is a maximum signal and minimal interference. The following are some basic rules to do it:

    • Elevate your router- simply raise your router off the floor, this will avoid large pieces of furniture that block your line of sight and prevent signals from being transmitted straight to the ground. For your apartment, place it near the ceiling near the floor. To avoid blocking the signal, consider your furniture placements.
    • Put the router in a central location– This will ensure that all rooms access the signal. Therefore, put it in the most central location in your apartment.
    • Minimize router obstructions– WiFi signals can be weakened by stone, metals, or tile, therefore, when choosing your router spot it should be near the most important WiFi zones in the apartment. Walls can also weaken WiFi, therefore, avoid walls if you can.

    Electronic devices can interfere with your WiFi signals, especially microwaves, whose radiation is close to that of WiFi signals.

    Apartment WiFi Router FAQs

    What Kind of Router Do I Need for My Apartment?

    For modern apartment living, you need a router that can support multiple high-consumption internet users who want to either stream or video conference at the same time.

    This includes routers that have high internet speeds, multiple bands and support large bandwidth internet plans.

    You also need a router with a good range that can cover all the key WiFi zones within the apartment. The required range is typically 2,500 sq ft for most apartment dwellers to reliably access the internet.

    Does It Make Sense to Buy a Mesh Router for A Large Apartment?

    Generally, mesh routers provide strong coverage. Therefore, if the apartment size is more than 2000 sq feet, installing a good mesh router is ideal for your space.

    Should I Buy a Modem Router Combo or Separate Units?

    This depends on your setup. The modem router combo combines the functionalities of a router and a modem. This does not necessarily translate to faster speeds or wider coverage.

    Nevertheless, you can purchase a modem-router combo if you have a two-bedroom apartment with a total area of 2000 to 2500 sq ft.

    Do Apartment Walls Interfere with WiFi Signal?

    Different building materials can block WiFi signals differently. WiFi signal will be weakened if it passes through thick walls, especially reinforced concrete.

    WiFi can get through walls but in doing so the signal typically weakens. The best situation is to have a line of sight between the router and your devices.

    For devices that are in other rooms ensure that the router is at a position where the signal passes through the lowest number of walls to get to your device, this will help you maximize the signal strength.
