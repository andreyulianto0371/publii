    Factors To Consider When Looking For The Best Modem

    Supported Speeds

    The supported speed of a cable modem is a significant factor to consider when weighing up different product options.

    For optimal performance, your cable modem should either surpass or match the speed of your internet plan.

    Often, the advertised speeds on modems don’t reflect the real-world performance so do make it a priority to at least match the modem speed with your internet plan speed.

    Opting for a modem that has a slower speed compared to your internet plan will create a bottleneck which leads to poor internet performance.

    Likewise, modems that do not feature the latest DOCSIS protocol or channel bonding capabilities, will not enable you to fully utilize high-speed internet plans.

    If you’re not maximizing the speed of your internet plan, you are effectively wasting money.

    To ensure you maximize performance and don’t waste money, you should verify that your modem choice is compatible with your ISP and is also capable of handling the speeds on your internet plan.

    DOCSIS Protocol

    The DOCSIS (Data Over Cable Service Interface Specification) Protocol is used by internet service providers to identify the maximum speeds for when the incoming ISP network is converted into a local household network.

    There have been various versions of the DOCSIS protocol, with DOCSIS 3.1 being the latest and DOCSIS 3.0 also widely available.

    The DOCSIS 3.1 protocol can support internet speeds of up to 10 Gbps. This is ten times faster than DOCSIS 3.0 which can handle speeds of up to 1 Gbps.

    Although some internet service providers such as Google Fiber, Verizon, and Xfinity offer household internet plans exceeding 1 Gbps, in 2024, most household internet plans won’t exceed a maximum speed of 1 Gbps.

    This means that modems featuring DOCSIS 3.0 should suffice for the majority of households.

    However, DOCSIS 3.1 is widely accepted to enhance performance on internet plans of 500 Mbps and above, through technology advances such as OFDM channels which can reduce latency.

    Another advantage of cable modems with DOCSIS 3.1 is that they are future-proof, as internet devices become faster and use more advanced technologies.

    While modems with DOCSIS 3.1 are recommended for optimal performance, DOCSIS 3.0 modems will operate well on most internet plans and will also be more budget-friendly.   

    Channel Bonding

    Channel bonding impacts the speed a cable modem can operate by combining channels to increase data-handling capacity.

    In your cable modem product research, you will have likely come across terms such as 16*4 or 32*8 channel bonding.

    The higher number refers to the total download channels, while the lower number refers to the upload channels.

    Higher channel numbers relate to faster internet speeds. For example, a setup of 32*8 with a device using DOCSIS 3.1 protocol, can handle internet speeds of up to 1 Gbps.

    You should be aware though that opting for high channel bonding won’t drastically improve performance on a lower-speed internet plan. For example, 32*8 channel bonding on a 150 Mbps plan would be unnecessary.

    For lower-speed plans, selecting a cable modem with 16*4 channel bonding would be more appropriate in terms of performance and cost.

    Compatibility With Your ISP

    Before understanding the technological aspects of a cable modem, you need to first work out if the device is compatible with your internet service provider.

    Although cable modems are compatible with most major ISPs, some may not be compatible with all. If it is not compatible, the device will not work properly.

    To check if a cable modem is compatible with your ISP. Search on your ISP’s website to find the list of all compatible third-party modems.

    Doing this quick and simple check can prevent you from investing in a modem that won’t work well with your ISP.

    Ports

    Understanding the ports available on a cable modem is important as it impacts the bandwidth capacity of the modem.

    Most cable modems have one LAN port and an additional service port. The LAN port connects the modem to the router’s WAN port or another ethernet-using device such as a computer or console.

    For the service port, cable modems feature a coaxial port to connect the device to the service line.

    DOCSIS 3.1 cable modems feature at least a single gigabit port, but some devices include two or more gigabit ports. The latest devices also include a 2.5G port which handles ultra-fast data transmission over 1 Gbps.

    Generally, we recommend cable modems that have at least two ethernet ports as they provide households with extra capacity to create wired internet connections.

    For example, if your household contains someone who regularly plays online games, having an extra ethernet port that can connect directly to the console can help maintain strong connectivity for the whole household.

    Separate Modem & Router vs. Modem/Router Combo

    Another factor to consider when choosing a cable modem is whether you’d like separate modem (and router) devices or a modem/router combo device.

    A combo device is easy to set up and maintain as both the functions of a modem and router and built into one device. Combo devices also come with a lower initial cost which can be ideal for households on a restricted budget.

    For best performance, control, and upgrading, we recommend purchasing separate modem and router devices.

    Purchasing separate devices will allow you to match the best router and cable modem to maximize your internet plan.

    Also, if a newer version of your modem or router becomes available, it is simpler to upgrade when having separate modem and router devices.

    How To Setup & Activate A Modem For Any ISP 

    Although the task may sound daunting to technophobes, setting up and activating a modem is a fairly straightforward process regardless of your ISP.

    For cable modems that are not combo devices, it is wise to set up your router first.

    Follow the steps below to set up your modem.

    1. Prepare key modem information – To quickly activate your device, you’ll need to know the device’s MAC address, serial number, and your ISP account ID. The MAC address and serial number are available on a sticker on your modem. Record this information on a piece of paper for ease.
    2. Disconnect your existing device and connect your new device – After you remove the ethernet and coaxial cables from your old modem, you’ll need to connect them to your new modem. Make sure both cables are fully connected.
    3. Double Check Internet Access – The majority of modems include five LEDs. Once you connect the device, all the lights should blink. Once the ‘Internet’ or ‘Online’ light stops blinking, you’re connected to the Internet.
    4. Activating a New Modem – Once the connection is established, the majority of ISPs need you to activate the device, using the key information from step one. You can use the phone app or web interface to do this.
    5. Return Rental Equipment – For those who are replacing a modem rental with a bought third-party modem, make sure you return your rental device once you’ve activated your new modem.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    However, if you have an internet plan that is higher than 500 Mbps, a DOCSIS 3.1 device is desirable as it contains technological advancements that improve security and maximize internet speeds.

    Is It Better To Get Separate Devices Over A Modem/Router Combo?

    This is down to personal preference. We generally recommend separate modem and router devices as they enable better customization and more advanced features.

    However, combo devices are easier to set up and take up less room.
