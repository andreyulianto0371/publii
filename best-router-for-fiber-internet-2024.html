    Factors To Consider When Looking For A Fiber Optic Router

    WiFi Speed & Wireless Protocol 

    Network speed is a crucial factor as it determines the rate of data transfer between a source and its destination. Faster speeds in fiber internet result in quicker downloading and uploading of data.

    Fiber optic internet can be available in different wireless protocols, namely WiFi 5 and WiFi 6, depending on the situation.

    WiFi 5, the previous standard protocol, was succeeded by WiFi 6 in 2019. WiFi 6 offers significant improvements, including up to 250% faster speeds and a slightly better range.

    However, it’s important to note that not all devices currently support WiFi 6. As a result, WiFi 5 remains a budget-friendly option and may be suitable if your devices are not compatible with the newer WiFi 6 standard.

    When considering whether to choose WiFi 5 or WiFi 6 for your home fiber connection, there are several factors to consider such as device compatibility, the need for WiFi 6 features, and budget.

    For those seeking even greater speed, tri-band routers with WiFi 6e can provide an immense amount of speed, particularly when paired with Gigabit plans.

    Looking ahead, WiFi 7 is the next significant advancement in wireless technology. It promises faster connections, lower latency, and improved management of a larger number of connections, building upon the successes of its predecessors.

    WiFi Range & Coverage

    When choosing a router, it’s important to consider the size and layout of your environment. However, keep in mind that the placement of the router might be limited by phone sockets or other factors.

    For optimal WiFi coverage, it is recommended to position your fiber optic router centrally in your home to ensure 360-degree coverage, especially in larger residences.

    Standard routers typically provide coverage within a range of 150 feet. However, certain router models offer extended coverage to reach larger areas.

    To eliminate dead zones and extend the WiFi signal in broader spaces, you can consider using WiFi extenders. These small devices can be strategically placed to boost the signal and enhance coverage.

    Investing in a fiber optic router with a generous WiFi range is highly recommended to minimize unexpected connectivity issues.

    While these routers may have a higher price tag initially, they provide long-term benefits by preventing the need for additional spending on extenders.

    Dual vs Tri-Band Routers

    Tri-band routers have gained popularity recently, but their benefits are mainly realized when connecting multiple devices to the network.

    These routers feature an additional band, typically a second 5 GHz band, in addition to the standard combination of 2.4 GHz and 5 GHz bands.

    The inclusion of this extra band helps alleviate congestion on the 5 GHz band, enabling more devices to enjoy faster connections.

    It’s important to consider that both 5 GHz bands on a tri-band router are generally faster than the 2.4 GHz band. However, they do have a shorter range compared to the 2.4 GHz band.

    If you have numerous devices scattered far away from the router, the benefits of a tri-band router may not be as significant for you.

    Ultimately, the decision to invest in a tri-band router depends on your specific needs. If you have a high number of devices and require faster connections, particularly near the router, a tri-band router can be a worthwhile investment.

    However, if you have fewer devices or need to connect devices from a distance, the advantages of a tri-band router may not be as pronounced for your situation.

    WiFi Routers vs Traditional Routers

    As the cost of whole-mesh routers continues to decrease, homeowners with larger residences should seriously consider investing in this technology to ensure reliable WiFi coverage.

    Even in compact spaces, factors such as the layout and construction materials of the home can lead to signal drops, dead zones, and slower speeds.

    Mesh networks offer an effective solution to address these issues by extending WiFi coverage using additional nodes strategically placed throughout the house.

    They can even expand coverage to areas such as garages or work sheds, providing a seamless experience throughout the entire property.

    On the other hand, traditional routers are placed in one room and all the devices can connect to it.

    This setup often leads to weaker coverage as the signals find it hard to penetrate the walls and connect to devices in other rooms.

    With the mesh network, the nodes placed throughout your home strengthen and extends the coverage to ensure you have a reliable and consistent connection.

    Whether you have a larger home or face connectivity challenges due to your home’s layout, investing in a mesh network can greatly improve WiFi coverage, eliminate dead zones, and mitigate issues such as slower speeds and latency.

    By embracing this technology, homeowners can enjoy a more robust and reliable wireless network throughout their entire living space.

    Fiber Technology Explained

    Fiber optic technology differs from traditional cable internet technology. While cable technology transfers electrical signals, fiber optic transports information via super-fast light technology where light particles travel down the cable by bouncing off the walls.

    A fiber optic cable is comprised of tiny glass strands which are thinner than human hair. Given the vast number of glass strands in one fiber optic cable, one cable alone can carry one million calls.

    Unlike a traditional coax cable connection which requires a modem to translate the analog ISP network into a digital network, fiber optic technology requires an Optical Network Terminal (ONT) to convert the light signals into digestible electrical signals for your router and other internet devices to understand.

    To install the ONT, you’ll need an existing AC power outlet at your home or office. If your ISP provides fiber optic connection, you cannot use a coax cable modem or router to access the network, as the devices won’t be able to interpret the light signals.

    How To Install Fiber Optic Internet?

    These steps are very similar to self-installing other types of the Internet but with a few important differences. Let’s take a closer look at each one:

    1. Locate your fiber network terminal.

    Unlike coax jacks or phone jacks which are commonly flat plates installed against walls, fiber terminals have a different appearance. These terminals are typically larger and blockier in size.

    They can vary in dimensions, ranging from slightly larger than a juice box to as big as a briefcase.

    During installation, fiber terminals are often placed in inconspicuous locations where a power outler is accessible, such as the corner of a room, inside a closet, or even in the garage.

    2. Connect the fiber terminal to the network box.

    The subsequent procedure involves connecting your network box or gateway to the fiber terminal. Generally, this is accomplished using an Ethernet cable, which should have been supplied along with the network box.

    Start by inserting one end of the Ethernet cable into the port on the fiber terminal, and then connect the other end to the corresponding port on the network box.

    The port on the network box is typically labeled as “ONT” or “Fiber Jack,” indicating its purpose in the setup.

    3. Plug in your network box.

    After successfully connecting your network box or gateway to the fiber terminal, proceed to connect the power cord to the network box and plug it into a power outlet.

    Similar to a router, the device’s indicator lights will illuminate and begin to flash, eventually transitioning to a steady, solid color.

    It’s important to note that the activation process for fiber service may take longer compared to other types of internet connections, often requiring up to 15 minutes or more for the lights to stabilize and indicate a fully operational state.

    4. Connect your device to the network box.

    To establish your WiFi network, you need to connect a device to your network box or gateway. This can be accomplished using a laptop, desktop computer, or even a mobile phone.

    In most cases, network boxes will automatically generate a default WiFi network. Instructions on how to connect to this network are typically provided either on a sticker affixed to the network box (often found on the bottom) or within the accompanying manual.

    If your network box does not generate a default WiFi network during the setup process, you can establish a direct connection by using an Ethernet cable.

    5. Set up your home WiFi network.

    The last step in configuring your fiber internet is customizing your home WiFi network name and password. This allows you to personalize and secure your wireless network.

    Fiber Routers FAQs

    What Kind of Router Do I Need for Fiber Internet?

    To optimize your fiber internet connection, it is crucial to have a high-speed WiFi router.

    When selecting a wireless router for fiber internet, consider several factors including the WiFi standard, throughput, device compatibility, and coverage capacity.

    Assessing these aspects will help ensure you choose a router that can fully support and maximize the capabilities of your fiber internet connection.

    Does Fiber Technology Use Modems?

    Fiber internet, specifically designed for data connections, does not require a traditional modem.

    Instead, it utilizes an Optical Network Terminal (ONT) at each endpoint to establish the connection.

    What Is The Difference Between Fiber and Cable Internet?

    Fibre technology uses glass strands that are flexible to transmit data using light signals, enabling faster, clearer, and long-distance transmission.

    The strands are protected and bundled together by layers of plastic for durability and insulation.

    On the other hand, cable internet transmits data using coaxial cables in the form of electrical signals.

    Coaxial cables are made up of a copper core that is shielded and insulated with layers of copper, aluminum, and plastic on the outer layer.
